Welcome to my final project.

This website uses a dataset of the top ~200 movies at the domestic (US) box office from 1980–2024. This data was collected from boxofficemojo.com and formatted to be uniform. The data is not adjusted for inflation, so keep that in mind. However, I do not plan on comparing years in terms of raw gross; rather, I will be comparing demographics within individual years compared to other years. For example: the share of gross made by PG-13 movies in 1990 ($493,297,842 / $4,521,606,303 ≈ 11%) versus 2019 ($2,511,252,426 / $11,303,749,848 ≈ 22%).

To demonstrate how this dataset functions, I have tracked the total grosses from the 12 months in a year. As you can see, the period of May, June, and July has the largest gross before the last 2 months of every year. Summer is a key period of blockbuster releases, where studios release the hottest popcorn movies each year. Jurassic Park and Avengers: Endgame, two movies that held the title of highest-grossing movie ever for certain periods, were released in June and the last weekend of April, respectively.

As for the winter months, studios release a high volume of awards-seeking movies at the end of the year, in time for the awards season that runs roughly December through March. Also, the holiday periods of Thanksgiving and Christmas/New Year’s so close to each other provide big windows for high-grossing releases. Titanic and Avatar, another two movies that once held the #1 all-time spot (Avatar retains this title today), were both released about a week before Christmas.
